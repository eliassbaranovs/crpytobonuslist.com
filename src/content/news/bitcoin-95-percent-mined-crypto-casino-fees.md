## The Core Story

The Bitcoin network reached a monumental and irreversible milestone on March 9. The 20 millionth coin entered circulation, pushing the total supply past the 95% mark of its mathematically enforced 21 million hard cap. This specific event occurred at block height 940,000, which was successfully validated and added to the blockchain by the mining pool Foundry USA, according to on-chain data from Mempool. The moment marks a critical turning point in Bitcoin's economic lifecycle, shifting the conversation from issuance to long-term sustainability.

This milestone is significant because it brings the network's core value proposition, absolute scarcity, into sharp focus. It took approximately 17 years of continuous operation and immense computational effort to mine the first 20 million BTC. In stark contrast, the final one million coins are programmed to be released over the next 116 years, with the final fractions, known as satoshis, expected to be mined around the year 2140. This tapering issuance schedule is the engine of Bitcoin's scarcity model, a feature that industry leaders are quick to highlight.

Thomas Perfumo, the chief economist at the major cryptocurrency exchange Kraken, positioned the event within this framework of digital rarity. He stated, “In a world of excess and abundance, Bitcoin stands as one of the few truly scarce assets. Unlike traditional currencies with unlimited supply, Bitcoin's maximum supply is mathematically bound.” Similarly, Simon Gerovich, founder of the Japan-based, Bitcoin-focused firm Metaplanet, suggested the mining of the final 1 million BTC would usher in “the era [when] true digital scarcity [begins].” While both Kraken and Metaplanet have direct financial interests in Bitcoin's success, their statements are grounded in the verifiable mechanics of the network's open-source code, which has run without interruption since 2009.

## The Numbers

The math doesn't lie, and the on-chain data paints a clear picture of a system undergoing a profound economic transition. Bitcoin's issuance has been front-loaded by design. Early miners received a massive 50 BTC for each block they validated. This reward was programmatically cut in half to 25 BTC in 2012, then to 12.5 BTC in 2016, and again to 6.25 BTC in 2020. The most recent "halving" event in April 2024 reduced the subsidy to just 3.125 BTC per block. This predictable reduction occurs every 210,000 blocks, or roughly every four years, and is the central mechanism forcing the network to mature.

This shrinking subsidy places immense pressure on the miners who secure the network. Their revenue is being systematically reduced, forcing them to rely more on the second component of their compensation: transaction fees. Data from Hashrate Index shows just how thin the margins have become. In late February, Hashprice, a key metric tracking daily mining revenue per unit of hashing power, fell below $30 per petahash per second per day. This level is considered at or below the operational breakeven point for many mining operations once electricity and hardware costs are considered.

The data also reveals that transaction fees are not yet sufficient to replace the lost subsidy revenue. A report from Hashrate Index noted that during the week of the milestone, miners collected an average of just 0.0192 BTC in fees per block. When compared against the new block subsidy of 3.125 BTC, it is clear that miner income remains overwhelmingly dependent on the issuance of new coins and the market price of BTC. The fee market currently represents a tiny fraction of total miner revenue, a reality that must change for the network to remain secure in the long term. This economic strain is fueling a strategic pivot, with publicly traded miners like Core Scientific and Bitfarms announcing over $43 billion in contracts related to artificial intelligence and high-performance computing over the past year.

Foundry USA, the mining pool that mined the 20 millionth coin, is one of the largest and most influential players in the North American mining scene. Their success in finding the block highlights the industrial scale that Bitcoin mining has reached, a far cry from the early days of CPU mining on personal computers.

The commentary from Kraken and Metaplanet provides insight into how established financial players view this milestone. Metaplanet, a firm that holds Bitcoin as a primary treasury asset, mirrors this sentiment through its founder, Simon Gerovich. For them, the shrinking supply of new coins is a bullish signal that reinforces their strategic allocation. Their perspective underscores the dual nature of Bitcoin as both a technological network and a financial asset.

The most dramatic story, however, is unfolding within the mining sector itself. The constant pressure of the halving cycle is forcing a rapid evolution. Companies are splitting into two camps. One group is doubling down on hyper-efficiency, seeking out the cheapest power contracts and the most advanced mining hardware to stay profitable on razor-thin margins. The other group is undergoing a radical transformation, reframing their facilities not as Bitcoin mines but as versatile data centers. Publicly traded miners including Core Scientific, Bitfarms, TeraWulf, CleanSpark, and Hut 8 have all announced significant pivots toward providing infrastructure for artificial intelligence and high-performance computing, which are higher-margin businesses. This shift is a direct response to the economic reality that pure-play Bitcoin mining is becoming an increasingly difficult business.

As the 3.125 BTC block reward dwindles further in subsequent halvings, the network's "security budget"—the total revenue paid to miners to prevent attacks—must increasingly come from the transaction fees paid by users. For the network to remain secure, miners must be compensated adequately for their operational costs. This means total fee revenue per block must eventually rise to a level comparable to today's subsidy.

For a crypto casino player, this translates into potentially higher costs for moving funds. To get a transaction confirmed quickly, a user must attach a fee that is attractive to miners. In a future where miners are almost entirely reliant on these fees, the baseline cost for a standard transaction is likely to be significantly higher than it is today. Small deposits and withdrawals could become less economical as the network fee might represent a larger percentage of the total transaction value.

During periods of high network congestion, such as a major market rally or the launch of a popular new on-chain application, the competition for block space intensifies dramatically. This creates a bidding war where users must pay higher fees to avoid long confirmation times. As the base level of required fee revenue increases, these peaks in demand will start from a higher floor, potentially leading to extreme fee spikes. Players will need to be more strategic about when they transact, perhaps choosing to move funds during off-peak hours or utilizing Layer 2 solutions to batch transactions and reduce costs. The days of reliably cheap on-chain BTC transactions may be numbered.

## What Happens Next

The path forward for the Bitcoin network is both predictable and uncertain. The code dictates a clear schedule: the next halving will occur around 2028, cutting the block subsidy once again to approximately 1.56 BTC. This relentless, programmatic tightening will continue until the last satoshi is mined around 2140. The predictable part is the supply shock. The uncertain part is how the demand side, specifically the transaction fee market, will respond to shoulder the security burden.

The immediate future will see an acceleration of the trends already underway in the mining industry. We can expect more consolidation as smaller, less efficient miners are squeezed out by operators with superior economies of scale and access to cheap power. The pivot to adjacent computing industries like AI will also continue. Mining companies will increasingly market themselves as specialized energy and data infrastructure providers, with Bitcoin mining being just one of several revenue streams. This diversification is a necessary hedge against the volatility of both the BTC price and the transaction fee market.

The central question for the next decade is whether organic use of the Bitcoin network will grow enough to generate a robust and stable fee market. For the network to be secure in 2032 and beyond, the sum of all transaction fees in a block must provide a compelling revenue incentive for an industrial-scale mining industry to continue its operations. If fees remain low and stagnant, the network's hashrate could decline, potentially leaving it more vulnerable. On-chain reality check: The success of this transition is not guaranteed. It hinges on Bitcoin evolving from primarily a store of value into a network that people are willing to pay a premium to use for settlement. The answer will determine the long-term health and security of the entire protocol.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why are Bitcoin transaction fees expected to rise in the long term?
Bitcoin's design includes a "halving" event every four years that cuts the reward for miners in half. As this "block subsidy" approaches zero, miners must rely on transaction fees to fund their operations. For the network to remain secure, total fees paid by users must rise to replace the lost subsidy income.

### How will higher BTC fees affect my crypto casino deposits and withdrawals?
Higher network fees mean a larger portion of your transaction will be paid to miners, not sent to its destination. This can make smaller deposits or withdrawals less cost-effective. During times of network congestion, fees can spike, meaning you may have to pay more for a fast confirmation or wait longer for your transaction to be processed.

### What does the 95% mined milestone mean for Bitcoin's scarcity?
Reaching the 20 million coin mark out of a total of 21 million highlights Bitcoin's finite supply. Unlike traditional currencies that can be printed indefinitely, Bitcoin has a fixed cap. This event reinforces its digital scarcity, which is a key part of its value proposition for many investors and users.

### What is 'Hashprice' and why is it important for Bitcoin miners?
Hashprice is a metric that measures the expected daily revenue a Bitcoin miner can earn for a specific amount of computing power (e.g., per petahash per second). When the Hashprice falls below a miner's cost of electricity and operations, they begin to lose money, which can force them to shut down their machines.
